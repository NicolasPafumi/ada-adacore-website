Abstract

It is an established fact that presidential elections have a significant impact on the stock market. Investors react to the uncertainty and potential policy changes that come with a new administration. Understanding these dynamics can help investors anticipate these movements and hedge their portfolios around election cycles. These insights also provide a window into the market's perceptions of different candidates and their proposed policies.
In this project, we explore how presidential elections influence company performance and investor sentiment over time. Using historical stock market data, election results, and prediction market trends, we look at how markets move as the likelihood of one candidate winning changes. We focus especially on how different sectors for example like technology, energy, healthcare, and finance, respond to the uncertainty and outcomes of election cycles.
Our goal is to show how politics and markets move together in a clear, data-driven way. By analyzing past elections and the upcoming 2024 race, we hope to uncover patterns that explain when, why, and how the market reacts.

Research questions

Can we describe an entire sector? Do sectors have any significance in terms of shared financial behaviour? are they affected differently? Which sectors are most affected by presidential elections? What characteristics of elections lead to increased volatility? Does the president's party differentiably impact certain sectors? To answer these questions, we will use historical stock market data and publicly available election data. We will also use prediction market data as proxy for the perceived probability of each candidate winning.

Estimators of Volatility

Returns tend to be centered around 0 as prices oscillate around a trend. During the 2008 crisis, we observe an increase in volatility, with more pronounced fluctuations. This pattern of high volatility could be indicative of what we expect around election dates as well, although less pronounced. We can quantify this volatility using standard deviation of returns over a rolling window. This estimate of volatility is referred to as historical volatility

Historical volatility

Volatility is the finance term for the standard deviation. It can be computed using usual unbiased standard deviation formula with a rolling window.

Formula:

$$\sigma_H = \sqrt{ \frac{1}{N-1} \sum_{t=1}^{N} (\bar{V}^2 - V_t^2) } $$

With \(N\) the selected window, e.g. \(t\) representing a day, \( \bar{V} \) the average price for the given window and \(V_t\) the price on a given \(t\) (e.g. a day).

Sectors typically follow the overall market trend, with increased volatility during events affecting the entire economy, such as the 2008 financial crisis or the August 2011 market fall. However, this simple visualization hints that sectors can exhibit different levels of volatility, responding to events that preferentially impact them. A good example is the energy sector that showed above-market volatility from 2005 to 2007, as conflicts in the Middle East and other factors influenced oil prices. Other sectors were largely unaffected during that period. Similarly, the years 2001-2003 saw increased volatility in the technology sector following the burst of the dot-com bubble, while other sectors remained relatively stable.

This already supports our hypothesis that sector-specific analysis is important to capture the full impact of elections. As candidates take different stances on issues like climate change, regulation of Big Tech or healthcare reform, sectors will be differentially affected!

while historical volatility is easy to compute and is typically used for long-term analysis and risk management, it has its limitations. It needs a sufficiently large window size to provide reliable estimates, which can smooth out the day-to-day fluctuations we aim to capture around election dates. Additionally, since it is only based on close prices, it ignores intraday price movements. In the context of elections, market reactions can be swift and get resolved within the same trading day. For these reasons, we use Parkinson's volatility estimator, which leverages high and low prices within the trading day to provide a more responsive measure

Parkinson volatility

Parkinson's volatility uses the highest and lowest price reached in a given time (e.g. a day) to compute volatility. It needs to be computed over a time window (e.g. a month) and yields accurate estimates with smaller values of \(N\), allowing to capture short-term variation without sacrificing reliability.

Formula :

$$\sigma_P = \sqrt{\frac{1}{4 \ln(2)} \cdot \frac{1}{N} \sum_{t=1}^{N} \left( \ln\left(\frac{H_t}{L_t}\right) \right)^2}$$

where $H_t$ and \(L_t\) are the high and low prices on day \(t\). The factor \( \frac{1}{4 \ln(2)} \) is derived from modeling returns as a geometric Brownian motion.

If it does, how do you represent entire sector

So far, we've used sector-specific ETFs as proxies for the performance and volatility of the entire sectors. This ETF family, called Select Sector SPDRs, is a well-established way to track sector performance. [what they are used for, in academia, to diversify portfolios] They each contain the companies of their respective sectors that appear in the S&P 500 index. Each company is weighted by its market capitalization, with around 50% of holding typically concentrated in the top 10 companies. This is a better way to track sector volatility than averaging the volatility of all companies in the sector for two reasons:

1. Large companies affect sector performance more than small companies. A non-weighted average would give equal importance to all companies. 2. Small companies have higher volatility than big companies. This volatility may not be due to sector sentiment but rather to the fact that small companies are traded in lower volumes and can be thus be affected by modest day-to-day trades. This would artificially inflate sector volatility.

Let us verify this by calculating volatility as an average of individual stock volatilities within each sector and comparing it to the ETF volatility.

[graph of parkinson’s estimated in 2 ways]

The histograms on the left show that on any specific day, the distribution of volatilities for companies within the same sector is right-skewed. Most companies have volatilities clustered around a central value, but a few outliers exhibit very high volatilities. This confirms our earlier assumption that small companies, more sensitive to day-to-day trading, will skew the average. In the healthcare sector, for example, the outlier is TLGT (Teligent Inc.) reaching a volatility 0f 0.36. At the time of measurement, Teligent was a small pharmaceutical company with a market capitalization of only $10 million.

It is thus no surprise that our naive sector volatility calculation consistently overestimates sector volatility as shown on the lineplots on the right. However, we are surprised to see that while the naive calculation is an overestimation, it nonetheless reproduces closely historical patterns over two decades and is not as noisy as we expected. Using the median instead of the mean to aggregate individual stock volatilities slightly improves the estimate, but it still remains an overestimation.

Event study

How does one determine how a specific event impacted the performance or volatility of a stock? Tools from event study methodology help us answer such questions. Our study will go as follows:

- Define an event window around Election Day, the event of interest: we consider both pre- and post-election windows as the dates of elections are known and changes in the market could even happen before as investors try to anticipate the outcome or hedge their positions. - Calculate realized returns or volatility over that window. - Compare these values to expected returns and volatility: event studies differ by how they define these expected, or normal, values. We will demonstrate two approaches. - Aggregate results across different sectors and election cycles and determine statistical significance.

Abnormal returns based event study

let us use abnormal returns to evaluate election impact. abnormal returns defined using alpha and beta, explain why:

By ignoring these factors, a naive model may misattribute as event-driven and abnormal returns that simply due to the sector's historical characteristics. We thus account for the relationship between sector returns and market returns using linear regression. The market model is defined as follows:

$$ R_{i,t} = \alpha_i + \beta_i R_{m,t} + \epsilon_{i,t} $$

where \( \alpha_i \) is the stock's alpha, \( \beta_i \) is the stock's beta, and \( \epsilon_{i,t} \) is the return not explained by the model, aka the abnormal return:

$$ AR_{i,t} = R_{i,t} - (\alpha_i + \beta_i R_{m,t}) $$

It is customary to estimate \( \alpha_i \) and \( \beta_i \) over a large window prior to the window of interest. In our example, we will define a 252-day estimation window, corresponding to one trading year. We can then aggregate these abnormal returns across our event window to obtain cumulative abnormal returns (CAR):

$$ CAR_{i,t} = \sum_{m \leq t} AR_{i,m} $$

Let us use this method to analyze the impact of the 2016 presidential election on different sectors.
